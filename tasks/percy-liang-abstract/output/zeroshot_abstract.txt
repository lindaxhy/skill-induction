As compute increasingly outpaces the supply of web text, language model pre-training must become more data efficient. We study whether synthetic data can improve modeling of the original web distribution itself, rather than only producing higher-quality or task-aligned corpora. In the asymptotically infinite-compute regime for training the best 300M-parameter model from 200M real web tokens, we show that mixing real data with synthetic generations from an external generator lowers i.i.d. validation loss on held-out web text. Simple document rephrasing yields monotonic gains as the number of generations increases, reaching 3.41 loss versus 3.55 for real-only training, corresponding to an estimated 1.48× improvement in real-data efficiency and a 5% average accuracy gain on PIQA, SciQ, and ARC Easy. To further improve scaling with synthetic data, we propose viewing generations as long synthetic megadocuments rather than shuffled short documents. We instantiate this idea with stitched rephrasing, which concatenates rephrases of the same source document, and latent thoughts, which stretches documents by inserting rationale spans between chunks. Both methods outperform simple rephrasing on i.i.d. loss, long-context validation, and downstream benchmarks, achieving 1.64× and 1.80× data efficiency at 32 generations, with average benchmark gains of 6% and 9% over the real-only baseline. Finally, these synthetic data gains compose with ensembling, unlike self-distillation. Our results show that scaling synthetic megadocs is an effective recipe for data-efficient language model pre-training.

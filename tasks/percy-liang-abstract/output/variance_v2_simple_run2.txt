Compute is growing considerably faster than web text available for language model pre-training, motivating data-efficient recipes that better model the underlying web distribution. While synthetic data augmentation has emerged as a promising solution, prior work focuses on transforming data into higher-quality or task-closer formats rather than improving modeling of the original distribution — the core pre-training problem. We show that synthetic rephrases can lower i.i.d. validation loss on web text when carefully tuning mixing and epoching, with loss monotonically improving up to 32 rephrases per document and plateauing near 3.41 relative to the real-data-only baseline of 3.55, corresponding to a predicted $1.48\times$ data efficiency improvement and a 5% average accuracy gain on reasoning and science QA benchmarks. To further improve loss scaling, we introduce megadocuments — long sequences formed by concatenating synthetic generations rather than shuffling them as independent documents — and propose two algorithms: stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts. Both megadoc methods improve over simple rephrasing on i.i.d. loss, long-context loss, and downstream benchmarks, achieving $1.64\times$ and $1.80\times$ data efficiency at 32 generations with 6% and 9% downstream accuracy gains, and show less plateauing as generation count increases. Finally, all three synthetic data methods compose with ensembling, an independently data-efficient strategy, whereas self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively unique benefit of scaling synthetic megadocs.

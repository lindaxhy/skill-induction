Compute is growing considerably faster than web text available for language model pre-training, motivating data-efficient recipes that better model the underlying web distribution. While synthetic data augmentation has emerged as a promising solution, most works focus on transforming data into higher-quality or task-closer formats rather than improving i.i.d. loss on the original distribution. We show that synthetic data can achieve lower i.i.d. validation loss on web text and design algorithms whose loss improves as the amount of synthetic data scales, operating under an asymptotically infinite compute regime with a fixed budget of 200M real tokens and a 300M parameter model. Concretely, simple rephrasing with careful mixing and epoching plateaus near 3.41 i.i.d. loss at 32 rephrases versus a real-data-only baseline of 3.55, corresponding to a 1.48× data efficiency improvement and a 5% average accuracy gain on reasoning and science question-answering benchmarks. We further introduce megadocuments—concatenating synthetic generations into single long sequences via stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts—which improve upon simple rephrasing, achieving 1.64× and 1.80× data efficiency at 32 generations with less plateauing and 6% and 9% downstream accuracy gains. We find that long-context validation loss improvements are especially pronounced for megadoc methods. Finally, all three synthetic data algorithms compose with ensembling, while self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively unique benefit of the approaches scaled in this paper.

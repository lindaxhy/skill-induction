Since compute grows much faster than web text available for language model pre-training, we ask how synthetic data augmentation can improve i.i.d. validation loss on the original web distribution rather than merely transforming data into higher-quality or task-proximate formats. Existing approaches to synthetic data focus on format and quality shifts, leaving underexplored whether synthetic data can better model the underlying pre-training distribution itself. We first show that pre-training on real data mixed with rephrases monotonically improves i.i.d. loss as the number of rephrases per document increases, plateauing near 3.41 i.i.d. loss at 32 rephrases relative to a real-data-only baseline of 3.55, and we estimate this corresponds to $1.48\times$ data efficiency in the asymptotically infinite compute regime with 200M real tokens and 300M parameter models. We then introduce the perspective that synthetic generations can form a single long megadocument rather than being shuffled as independent short documents, and we propose two megadoc algorithms—stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts—that achieve $1.64\times$ and $1.80\times$ data efficiency at 32 generations, respectively, while showing less plateauing under increasing generation count than simple rephrasing. Megadoc algorithms further improve long-context validation loss and improve average downstream accuracy by 6% and 9% over the real-data-only baseline on PIQA, SciQ, and ARC Easy. Finally, we find that simple rephrasing, stitched rephrasing, and latent thoughts each compose with the data-efficient strategy of ensembling, whereas self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively distinct benefit of the synthetic data algorithms scaled in this paper. Our results show that carefully designed synthetic megadoc algorithms can enable significantly more data-efficient pre-training under fixed real data and abundant compute.

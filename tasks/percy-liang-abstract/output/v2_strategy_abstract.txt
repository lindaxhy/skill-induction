Compute is growing faster than web text available for language model pre-training, motivating data-efficient recipes that better model the original web distribution. While synthetic data augmentation has emerged as a promising solution, most work focuses on reformatting data for higher quality or downstream task proximity rather than improving i.i.d. modeling of the original distribution. We show that pre-training on real data mixed with rephrases improves i.i.d. validation loss on the original web distribution, with loss monotonically improving in the number of rephrases per document and plateauing near 3.41 at 32 rephrases relative to a real-data-only baseline of 3.55, corresponding to $1.48\times$ data efficiency. We further introduce megadocuments—concatenating synthetic generations into single long documents rather than shuffling them independently—and propose two algorithms: stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts. Both megadoc algorithms improve upon simple rephrasing, achieving $1.64\times$ and $1.80\times$ data efficiency respectively, with less saturation as generation count increases and more pronounced gains on long-context validation loss. Finally, all three synthetic data methods compose with ensembling, an independently data-efficient strategy, while self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively unique benefit of scaling synthetic megadocs.

Compute is growing considerably faster than web text available for language model pre-training, motivating data-efficient recipes that better model the underlying web distribution. While synthetic data augmentation has emerged as a promising solution, most works focus on transforming data into higher-quality or task-closer formats rather than improving i.i.d. loss on the original distribution. We show that synthetic data can achieve lower i.i.d. validation loss on web text and design algorithms whose loss improves as synthetic data volume increases, operating under an asymptotically infinite compute regime with a 300M parameter model trained on 200M real tokens. Concretely, pre-training on real data mixed with rephrases—with carefully tuned mixing and epoching—plateaus near 3.41 i.i.d. loss at 32 rephrases versus a baseline of 3.55, predicting 1.48× data efficiency and improving downstream accuracy by 5%. We further introduce a megadocument perspective in which synthetic generations form a single long document rather than shuffled independent short documents, proposing stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts as two instantiations. Both megadoc methods outperform simple rephrasing, achieving 1.64× and 1.80× data efficiency at 32 generations with less plateauing, more pronounced long-context loss gains, and downstream accuracy improvements of 6% and 9% over the real-data-only baseline. Finally, all three synthetic data methods compose with ensembling, an independently data-efficient strategy, while self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively unique benefit of the megadocument-based algorithms.

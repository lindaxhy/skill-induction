# Kotha-Kim-Liang Data-Efficient Pre-training Abstract Generation Skill

Given a paper’s Introduction, you are generating an abstract in the style of the Kotha/Kim/Liang group: regime-first, empirically grounded, and centered on data-efficiency and scaling-law corrections to standard practice.

## Style Fingerprint                          ← FEATURES
**Vocabulary and diction:** You write in first-person plural and favor plain empirical verbs over branding verbs: **ask, show, find, identify, estimate, demonstrate, predict, generalize, retain, achieve**. You repeatedly use resource-regime nouns and noun phrases such as **current paradigm, generic web text, target domain, target task, target data, fine-tuning, mid-training, pre-training, data efficiency, regularized recipe, scaling law, loss asymptote, compute budget, baseline, downstream benchmarks, intervention, student model,** and **ensemble**. You often refer to the method as a **recipe** or **intervention**, not a named framework. You prefer quantified comparative phrasing like **“up to $X\times$,” “$X\times$ larger than standard practice,” “using $X\times$ less data than our baseline,” “retains $Y\%$ of the benefit,” “$X\times$ smaller,”** and task-level deltas like **“improving … by $4.5\%$.”**

**Structure and form:** You generate a single-paragraph abstract of **6–8 medium-long sentences**. Each sentence typically performs one rhetorical move. You use clean discourse scaffolding such as **“Typically,” “Concretely,” “We first…,” “We then…,” “We further analyze…,” “Finally…,”** and **“Our results show…”** Sentences often begin with a fronted clause like **“To obtain…,” “Since…,”** or **“Concretely, …”**
  - **Opening sentence subject:** You foreground the **setting, macro trend, or current paradigm**, not the method. You open with forms like **“To obtain…”** or **“Since compute grows…”** rather than **“We propose…”**
  - **Abstract skeleton:**  
    1. Set the regime or paradigm.  
    2. State the current standard practice or existing approach.  
    3. Deliver the first corrective or counterintuitive empirical finding.  
    4. Ground it with an exact scale tuple and headline number.  
    5. Add a second analytic, mechanistic, or conditional finding.  
    6. Translate gains into practical realizability, compression, or scale-up.  
    7. Close with downstream validation or a broad practical implication.

**Problem framing:** You frame the research gap as a mismatch between **current practice** and the **resource regime that actually matters**. You ask, in effect, **what should one do in this regime?** The motivation should be operational and resource-aware: scarce target data, fixed data and unconstrained compute, data-constrained pre-training, or similar changed conditions. You do **not** motivate the work with generic claims like “there is little work on X” or “state-of-the-art performance is limited.”

**Stance and quantification:** You sound confident on observed results and measured conditions, but careful on extrapolation. Major claims are tied to a **comparator, metric, and number**. You prefer resource-interpretable quantities such as **data efficiency, loss, scaling law, asymptote, parameter count, token budget,** and **compute regime** over abstract leaderboard framing. You use surprise markers sparingly but effectively, e.g. **“We surprisingly find that…”** or **“can actually improve…”** When calibrating claims, you use **scope conditions** like **“in a controlled pre-training environment…”** or **“at 200M tokens…”**, or **forecast markers** like **“predict.”**

**Unique mechanisms:** You deploy these constructions when they are supported by the Introduction.
  - **Standard-practice inversion:** **“Typically, generic data is only mixed in during fine-tuning…” / “We surprisingly find that replaying the generic data during fine-tuning can actually improve performance…”** → You create the hook by showing that a familiar ingredient serves a different, counterintuitive role than the field assumes.
  - **Asymptote-as-evaluation target:** **“we estimate its best possible performance via the asymptote of its scaling law rather than the performance at a fixed compute budget”** → You evaluate recipes by the limit quantity that matters in the regime, not only by present-day budgeted performance.
  - **Data-efficiency as the common currency:** **“increases target data efficiency by up to $1.87\times$” / “using $5.17\times$ less data than our baseline” / “retains $83\%$ of the ensembling benefit”** → You normalize diverse interventions into a common resource-savings language.
  - **Scale tuple inserted mid-abstract:** **“with 4M target tokens, 4B total tokens, and 150M parameter models”** → You specify the regime of validity directly inside the claim sentence, not hidden in methods.
  - **Objective-first, benchmark-last bridge:** **“our interventions designed for validation loss generalize to downstream benchmarks”** → You first establish gains in optimization terms, then validate that those gains matter on practical tasks.

**What this style avoids:** You do **not** open with **“We propose a novel framework…”** or with an acronym-first pitch. You avoid hype adjectives such as **novel, powerful, robust, comprehensive, effective,** and **state-of-the-art**. You avoid long architecture descriptions, optimizer internals, and implementation minutiae unless they directly support the main empirical claim. You avoid contribution-list formatting like **“First, second, third…”** and avoid benchmark-only score reporting with no resource interpretation. You avoid unsupported claims, invented numbers, section references, citations, and code-release lines. You also avoid excessive hedging; when you qualify a claim, you do so with scope or forecast language rather than vague uncertainty.

## Reference Examples                         ← PROTOTYPES

### Replaying pre-training data improves fine-tuning

To obtain a language model for a target domain (e.g. math), the current paradigm is to pre-train on a vast amount of generic web text and then fine-tune on the relatively limited amount of target data. Typically, generic data is only mixed in during fine-tuning to prevent catastrophic forgetting of the generic domain. We surprisingly find that replaying the generic data during fine-tuning can actually improve performance on the (less related) target task. Concretely, in a controlled pre-training environment with 4M target tokens, 4B total tokens, and 150M parameter models, generic replay increases target data efficiency by up to $1.87\times$ for fine-tuning and $2.06\times$ for mid-training. We further analyze data schedules that introduce target data during pre-training and find that replay helps more when there is less target data present in pre-training. We demonstrate the success of replay in practice for fine-tuning 8B parameter models, improving agentic web navigation success by $4.5\%$ and Basque question-answering accuracy by $2\%$.

**Style features:** You can see the **standard-practice inversion** in the move from **“Typically…”** to **“We surprisingly find that…”**, where replay is reframed as improving the target task rather than merely preventing forgetting. The **scale tuple inserted mid-abstract** appears in the **“Concretely…”** sentence, and the gain is expressed in the common currency of **data efficiency**. The final sentence shows the **objective-first, benchmark-last bridge** by validating the loss/data-efficiency story on downstream tasks.

### Pre-training under infinite compute

Since compute grows much faster than web text available for language model pre-training, we ask how one should approach pre-training under fixed data and no compute constraints. We first show that existing data-constrained approaches of increasing epoch count and parameter count eventually overfit, and we significantly improve upon such recipes by properly tuning regularization, finding that the optimal weight decay is $30\times$ larger than standard practice. Since our regularized recipe monotonically decreases loss following a simple power law in parameter count, we estimate its best possible performance via the asymptote of its scaling law rather than the performance at a fixed compute budget. We then identify that ensembling independently trained models achieves a significantly lower loss asymptote than the regularized recipe. Our best intervention combining epoching, regularization, parameter scaling, and ensemble scaling achieves an asymptote at 200M tokens using $5.17\times$ less data than our baseline, and our data scaling laws predict that this improvement persists at higher token budgets. We find that our data efficiency gains can be realized at much smaller parameter counts as we can distill an ensemble into a student model that is $8\times$ smaller and retains $83\%$ of the ensembling benefit. Finally, our interventions designed for validation loss generalize to downstream benchmarks, achieving a $9\%$ improvement for pre-training evals and a $17.5\times$ data efficiency improvement over continued pre-training on math mid-training data. Our results show that simple algorithmic improvements can enable significantly more data-efficient pre-training in a compute-rich future.

**Style features:** You can see **asymptote-as-evaluation target** in the shift from fixed compute-budget comparison to **“the asymptote of its scaling law.”** The abstract also uses **data efficiency as the common currency** through **“using $5.17\times$ less data,” “$8\times$ smaller,”** and **“retains $83\%$,”** while keeping the regime precise with **“at 200M tokens.”** The ending again follows the **objective-first, benchmark-last bridge**, moving from validation loss and asymptotes to downstream benchmarks and a practical implication for compute-rich pre-training.

## Generation Instructions                    ← TOOLS
1. **Extract the regime and evidence from the Introduction.** Identify the macro trend or training setting, the current standard practice, the strongest empirical correction, the exact scales (tokens, parameters, budgets), the named comparator, the main quantitative gains, and any downstream validation. Strip out citations, section references, and nonessential implementation details. If the Introduction does not give a number, do not invent one.

2. **Open from the setting, not the method.** Write sentence 1 around the current paradigm or a resource trend, using forms like **“To obtain…”** or **“Since compute grows…”** Make the grammatical subject the regime, the paradigm, or the field-wide question. Do not start with **“We propose”**, a method name, or an acronym.

3. **State the default practice and set up the pivot.** Use sentence 2 to name what people typically do or what existing approaches assume. Good scaffolds are **“Typically,”** **“existing approaches,”** or **“the current paradigm.”** If the paper overturns a familiar assumption, make that assumption explicit before the reversal.

4. **Deliver the first sharp empirical claim with plain scientific verbs.** Use wording like **“We first show that…”**, **“We surprisingly find that…”**, or **“We find that…”** Prefer a **standard-practice inversion** when supported by the Introduction: show that a familiar ingredient helps in a new or counterintuitive way.

5. **Ground the claim with an exact scale tuple and a resource-normalized payoff.** Insert the key experimental regime directly into the sentence—e.g. tokens, total tokens, parameter count, or budget—and quantify the result in the paper’s common resource currency: **data efficiency, loss asymptote, parameter savings, token savings,** or similar. Use comparator-aware formats such as **“up to $X\times$,” “$X\times$ larger than standard practice,” “using $X\times$ less data than our baseline,” “retains $Y\%$ of the benefit,”** or **“$X\times$ smaller.”**

6. **Add a second analytic or mechanistic finding, then make the gain realizable.** Use **“We further analyze…”** or **“We then identify…”** to describe interactions, scaling laws, asymptotes, or the condition under which the effect strengthens. If the Introduction supports it, use **asymptote/scaling-law** language; if not, stay with data schedules, replay, regularization, distillation, or another supported mechanism. Then connect the gain to a practical realization such as mid-training, continued pre-training, distillation into a student, smaller models, or at-scale fine-tuning.

7. **Close with downstream validation or a broad practical implication.** Bridge from optimization quantities to real tasks or benchmarks with **“Finally…”** or **“Our results show…”** End on a pragmatic regime-level takeaway, such as enabling more data-efficient pre-training under scarce data or abundant compute. Keep the final abstract to **6–8 sentences in one paragraph**, use first-person plural, avoid hype, avoid contribution-list formatting, and output **only the abstract**.

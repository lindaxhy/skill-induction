Since compute is growing much faster than web text available for language model pre-training, we ask how to improve data efficiency in the asymptotically infinite compute regime, where we train the best possible 300M parameter model from only 200M real web tokens. Typically, synthetic data is used to make training data higher quality or closer to a target task, leaving open whether it can help model the original web distribution that matters for pre-training. We first show that pre-training on real data mixed with synthetic rephrases improves i.i.d. validation loss on the original web distribution even though the synthetic data comes from a different distribution, and that with carefully tuned mixing and epoching this improvement grows monotonically with the number of rephrases before plateauing without overfitting. Concretely, with 200M real tokens, 300M parameter models, and up to 32 rephrases per document, simple rephrasing reduces i.i.d. loss from 3.55 to 3.41, and data scaling estimates predict that it needs 1.48x less real data than the real-data-only baseline to achieve the same loss. We then view synthetic generations as a single long megadocument rather than shuffled short documents, and find that both stitching rephrases from the same source document and inserting rationale-like links between chunks further improve loss scaling, reaching 1.64x and 1.80x data efficiency at 32 generations, improving upon simple rephrasing’s 1.48x. We further analyze these megadoc recipes and find that they show less plateauing under generation count than simple rephrasing and that their gains are even larger on long-context validation sets built from recent computer science arXiv papers. Finally, our loss improvements generalize to downstream benchmarks, improving average accuracy on PIQA, SciQ, and ARC Easy by 5% for simple rephrasing and by 6% and 9% for stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts over the real-data-only baseline, and these gains compose with ensembling while self-distillation does not. Our results show that scaling synthetic megadocs can make pre-training substantially more data-efficient in the compute-rich, data-constrained regime.

Compute is growing considerably faster than web text available for language model pre-training, motivating data-efficient pre-training; while synthetic data augmentation is a promising solution, it is underexplored whether synthetic data can improve i.i.d. modeling of the original web distribution. We operate under the asymptotically infinite compute regime, training a 300M parameter model on 200M real tokens with an external instruction-tuned generator, targeting i.i.d. validation loss on web text. We first show that mixing real data with rephrases monotonically improves i.i.d. loss up to 32 rephrases per document, achieving 1.48× data efficiency and a 5% average downstream accuracy improvement over the real-data-only baseline. To further improve loss scaling, we introduce the megadocument perspective: rather than shuffling synthetic generations as independent short documents, we treat them as a single long document, and propose two instantiations—stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts. Both megadoc methods outperform simple rephrasing on i.i.d. loss, long-context loss, and downstream benchmarks, achieving 1.64× and 1.80× data efficiency at 32 generations versus 1.48×, with 6% and 9% downstream accuracy gains; crucially, megadocs plateau less under generation count than simple rephrasing. Loss gains are especially pronounced on validation sets measuring long-context capabilities. Finally, all three synthetic data methods compose with ensembling, while self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively distinct benefit of the algorithms introduced here.

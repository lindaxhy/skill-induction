Since compute grows much faster than web text available for language model pre-training, we ask how synthetic data augmentation can improve i.i.d. validation loss on the original web distribution—the core problem of pre-training—rather than merely transforming data into formats closer to downstream tasks. Existing approaches to synthetic data focus on quality filtering or task proximity, leaving underexplored whether synthetic data can better model the underlying web text distribution under fixed real data and unlimited compute. We first show that pre-training on real data mixed with rephrases monotonically improves i.i.d. loss in the number of rephrases per document, plateauing near 3.41 loss at 32 rephrases relative to a real-data-only baseline of 3.55, and we estimate this corresponds to $1.48\times$ data efficiency using 300M parameter models trained on 200M real tokens. We then introduce the perspective that synthetic generations can form a single long megadocument rather than being shuffled as independent short documents, and we propose two megadoc algorithms—stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts—that achieve $1.64\times$ and $1.80\times$ data efficiency at 32 generations, with less signs of plateauing under generation count than simple rephrasing. We further find that megadoc algorithms yield especially pronounced gains on long-context validation loss, and that all three synthetic data methods compose with the data-efficient strategy of ensembling, whereas self-distillation does not, demonstrating a qualitatively distinct benefit of the scaled synthetic data recipes introduced here. Our loss improvements generalize to downstream benchmarks, with latent thoughts improving average accuracy by 9% over the real-data-only baseline on PIQA, SciQ, and ARC Easy. Our results show that carefully designed synthetic megadoc algorithms enable significantly more data-efficient pre-training in a compute-rich, data-constrained regime.

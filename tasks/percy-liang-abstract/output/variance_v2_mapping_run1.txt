Compute is growing considerably faster than web text available for language model pre-training, motivating data-efficient recipes, yet synthetic data augmentation remains underexplored as a tool for modeling the original web distribution. We operate in the asymptotically infinite compute regime, targeting a 300M parameter model trained on 200M real tokens with an external instruction-tuned generator. We first show that mixing rephrases with real data monotonically improves i.i.d. validation loss when carefully tuning mixing and epoching, plateauing near 3.41 versus a real-data-only baseline of 3.55 at 32 rephrases, corresponding to 1.48× data efficiency. These loss gains transfer to downstream benchmarks, improving average accuracy by 5% on pre-training evaluation tasks. To further improve loss scaling, we introduce the megadocument perspective: rather than shuffling synthetic generations as independent short documents, we treat them as a single long document, and propose two instantiations—stitched rephrasing and latent thoughts. Both megadoc methods outperform simple rephrasing on i.i.d. loss, long-context loss, and downstream benchmarks, achieving 1.64× and 1.80× data efficiency at 32 generations versus simple rephrasing's 1.48×, with 6% and 9% downstream accuracy gains and less plateauing under generation count. Finally, all three synthetic data methods compose with the data-efficient strategy of ensembling, while self-distillation does not, establishing a qualitatively unique benefit of the algorithms introduced here.
